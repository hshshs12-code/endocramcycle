Catecholamines

pathway🎥 Office hour · Slide 5 ↗

Catecholamine synthesis

Phenylalanine → Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine. Cofactors are the whole point: Phe-hydroxylase and Tyrosine-hydroxylase (rate-limiting) both need BH₄; DOPA decarboxylase (AADC) needs B₆; dopamine β-hydroxylase needs vitamin C; PNMT (NE→Epi) needs SAM and is induced by adrenal cortisol (why the medulla sits next to the cortex).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.11 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Which enzyme catalyzes the rate-limiting step of catecholamine synthesis?
Tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA) is rate-limiting and requires BH₄.
2. Tyrosine hydroxylase and phenylalanine hydroxylase both require which cofactor?
Both are BH₄-dependent aromatic hydroxylases.
3. Which enzyme/cofactor pair converts norepinephrine to epinephrine?
PNMT methylates NE→Epi using SAM; it is induced by cortisol.
4. Dopamine β-hydroxylase requires which vitamin as a cofactor?
DBH (dopamine → norepinephrine) is a copper enzyme that uses ascorbate.
5. Why is PNMT concentrated in the adrenal medulla?
Cortisol draining from the cortex through the medulla induces PNMT (NE→Epi).
6. Which enzyme converts L-DOPA to dopamine?
AADC (DOPA decarboxylase) uses vitamin B₆.
7. Dopamine β-hydroxylase is unusual because it is located?
DBH is the only catecholamine-synthesis enzyme that works inside the vesicle.
8. Metyrosine (α-methyltyrosine), used before pheochromocytoma surgery, inhibits?
Blocking the rate-limiting TH reduces catecholamine production.
9. Tyrosine (the catecholamine precursor) can be made from which amino acid?
PAH converts Phe → Tyr.
10. What is the immediate product of tyrosine hydroxylase?
Tyr → L-DOPA is the rate-limiting step.
buzzword🎥 Office hour · Slide 5 ↗

Catecholamine degradation & pheochromocytoma

Catecholamines are inactivated by MAO (needs FAD) and COMT (needs SAM). The urinary end-products are what you measure: VMA and metanephrines — not the parent hormones. ⭐ Pheochromocytoma → ↑VMA, ↑metanephrines (episodic hypertension, headache, palpitations, sweating).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.14 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Catecholamines are degraded by which two enzymes?
Monoamine oxidase (MAO) and catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT).
2. Which cofactor does monoamine oxidase (MAO) require?
MAO uses FAD; COMT uses SAM.
3. What is the best urinary screen for pheochromocytoma?
Measure the degradation products — VMA and metanephrines.
4. Classic pheochromocytoma presentation?
Catecholamine surges → the paroxysmal '5 P' picture.
5. Pheochromocytoma is classically associated with which syndromes?
Pheo is a feature of MEN2A/2B, VHL, and NF1.
6. COMT requires which cofactor?
COMT methylates catecholamines using SAM.
7. The major urinary metabolite of dopamine is?
HVA is the dopamine metabolite; VMA is from NE/Epi.
8. Metanephrine is the COMT metabolite of?
Epinephrine → metanephrine; norepinephrine → normetanephrine.
9. Which neural-crest childhood tumor raises urinary HVA and VMA?
Neuroblastoma secretes catecholamine metabolites.
10. Where does MAO act relative to the neuron?
MAO on mitochondria degrades intraneuronal amines; COMT is extraneuronal.

BH₄ & aromatic hydroxylases

concept🎥 Office hour · Slide 5 ↗

BH₄-dependent aromatic hydroxylases

BH₄ (tetrahydrobiopterin) is the shared cofactor for the three aromatic amino-acid hydroxylases: phenylalanine hydroxylase (Phe→Tyr), tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr→L-DOPA), and tryptophan hydroxylase (→serotonin/melatonin). One cofactor loss hits catecholamines AND serotonin.
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.12 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. BH₄ is the cofactor for which class of enzymes?
Phenylalanine, tyrosine, and tryptophan hydroxylases.
2. Which reaction does NOT use BH₄?
DOPA decarboxylase (AADC) uses vitamin B₆, not BH₄.
3. Tryptophan hydroxylase (BH₄-dependent) is rate-limiting for synthesis of?
It begins serotonin (and downstream melatonin) synthesis.
4. A single BH₄ deficiency reduces levels of which neurotransmitters?
BH₄ is shared by tyrosine and tryptophan hydroxylases.
5. Phenylalanine hydroxylase converts phenylalanine to?
Phe → Tyr (BH₄-dependent); its loss causes classic PKU.
6. BH₄ is synthesized from which precursor?
GTP cyclohydrolase I is the rate-limiting step of BH₄ synthesis.
7. Besides the aromatic hydroxylases, which enzyme also requires BH₄?
NOS uses BH₄ as a cofactor.
8. The rate-limiting enzyme of BH₄ synthesis is?
GTP cyclohydrolase I controls de-novo BH₄ production.
9. Which product does tyrosine hydroxylase generate?
TH: tyrosine → L-DOPA (BH₄-dependent, rate-limiting).
10. A BH₄ deficiency causes hyperphenylalaninemia because BH₄ is needed for?
Without BH₄, PAH can't convert Phe → Tyr → phenylalanine accumulates.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 12 ↗

BH₄ deficiency (non-classic PKU)

Looks like PKU (developmental delay, musty urine, hypopigmentation) but phenylalanine hydroxylase activity is normal. The defect is in BH₄ recycling/synthesis — classically dihydropteridine reductase (regenerates BH₄). ⭐ OH question: normal PAH → check dihydropteridine reductase. Also depletes dopamine/serotonin, so it is not corrected by a low-Phe diet alone.
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.12 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. A child has PKU-like features but NORMAL phenylalanine-hydroxylase activity. The defect is in?
Normal PAH → suspect BH₄ recycling/synthesis, classically dihydropteridine reductase.
2. Dihydropteridine reductase does what?
It recycles the oxidized cofactor back to active BH₄.
3. Why is a low-phenylalanine diet alone insufficient in BH₄ deficiency?
Neurotransmitter deficiency persists — give BH₄ + L-DOPA/5-HTP.
4. Classic (PAH-deficiency) PKU findings include?
Phenylketones give the musty odor; low tyrosine → hypopigmentation.
5. Treatment of BH₄ deficiency includes?
Replace the cofactor and bypass the neurotransmitter block.
6. GTP cyclohydrolase I deficiency classically causes?
A BH₄-synthesis defect depletes dopamine/serotonin.
7. DOPA-responsive dystonia is best treated with?
Bypassing the BH₄-dependent step restores dopamine.
8. Which neurotransmitters are depleted in BH₄ deficiency?
BH₄ is shared by tyrosine and tryptophan hydroxylases.
9. Why must classic PKU be diagnosed and treated in the newborn period?
Early phenylalanine restriction preserves neurodevelopment.
10. Sapropterin (synthetic BH₄) helps in which patients?
It provides the missing/limiting cofactor.

Steroid hormone synthesis

pathway🎥 Office hour · Slide 5 ↗

Steroidogenesis & StAR

All steroids start from cholesterol. StAR shuttles cholesterol into the mitochondrion (rate-limiting, ACTH-stimulated); desmolase (side-chain cleavage, CYP11A1) makes pregnenolone. From there the three vertical tracks give mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), glucocorticoids (cortisol), and sex steroids (androgens→estrogens).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.17 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Rate-limiting step of steroidogenesis?
StAR delivers cholesterol to the inner mitochondrial membrane (ACTH-stimulated).
2. Which enzyme converts cholesterol to pregnenolone?
Desmolase makes pregnenolone, the first committed steroid.
3. ACTH primarily stimulates which step of steroidogenesis?
ACTH drives the early rate-limiting step.
4. All steroid hormones are derived from?
Cholesterol is the common precursor.
5. The three classes of adrenal cortical steroids are?
Zona glomerulosa/fasciculata/reticularis → aldosterone/cortisol/androgens.
6. The final enzyme in aldosterone synthesis is?
CYP11B2 in the zona glomerulosa makes aldosterone.
7. Aldosterone is synthesized in which adrenal zone?
Glomerulosa = mineralocorticoids (salt).
8. Cortisol is synthesized primarily in the?
Fasciculata = glucocorticoids (sugar).
9. Adrenal androgens (DHEA) are made mainly in the?
Reticularis = androgens (sex).
10. Which enzyme is deficient in the most common form of CAH (blocking both cortisol and aldosterone)?
21-hydroxylase deficiency = ~90% of CAH.
buzzword🎥 Office hour · Slide 5 ↗

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)

A block below ACTH → low cortisol → ↑ACTH → adrenal hyperplasia and shunting of precursors. Memorize by BP & virilization: 21-hydroxylase deficiency (90%): ↓cortisol/aldosterone, salt-wasting, hypotension, virilization. 11β-hydroxylase: ↑11-deoxycorticosterone → hypertension + virilization. 17α-hydroxylase: hypertension + NO sex hormones (ambiguous/female external genitalia).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.21 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Most common enzyme deficiency in congenital adrenal hyperplasia?
21-hydroxylase deficiency accounts for ~90%.
2. Why does CAH cause adrenal hyperplasia?
Cortisol block → ↑ACTH drives hyperplasia and precursor buildup.
3. 21-hydroxylase deficiency classically presents with?
Loss of cortisol AND aldosterone → salt-wasting; shunted precursors → androgens.
4. 11β-hydroxylase deficiency causes hypertension because of accumulation of?
DOC has mineralocorticoid activity → Na⁺/water retention → HTN.
5. Which CAH gives hypertension WITH virilization?
11β → ↑DOC (HTN) plus androgen excess (virilization).
6. A female newborn with ambiguous genitalia and salt-wasting most likely has which enzyme deficiency?
Classic salt-wasting CAH is 21-hydroxylase deficiency.
7. Which precursor is elevated and diagnostic in 21-hydroxylase deficiency?
17-OH-progesterone builds up behind the 21-hydroxylase block.
8. Treatment of 21-hydroxylase-deficiency CAH is?
Replacing cortisol lowers ACTH and stops the precursor shunt.
9. CAH is inherited in which pattern?
Most CAH enzyme deficiencies are autosomal recessive.
10. Which CAH form has hypertension but NO virilization?
17α → ↑mineralocorticoid precursors (HTN) with absent sex steroids.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 10 ↗

17α-hydroxylase deficiency

OH question: 15-yr-old, primary amenorrhea, hypertension, ↑Na⁺, low 17-α-progesterone, absent secondary sexual characteristics, 46,XX. Block → no cortisol & no sex steroids, but ↑ mineralocorticoid precursors (DOC). ⭐ Expect ↑ACTH, ↑deoxycorticosterone, ↓testosterone / ↓estrogen.
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.21 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. 17α-hydroxylase deficiency affects blood pressure and sex hormones how?
Block → ↑mineralocorticoid precursors (HTN) and no androgens/estrogens.
2. A 46,XY individual with 17α-hydroxylase deficiency presents phenotypically as?
No androgen synthesis → undervirilized (female-appearing) genitalia.
3. Which precursor accumulates and drives the hypertension in 17α deficiency?
↑DOC → mineralocorticoid effect → HTN, hypokalemia, ↑Na⁺.
4. A 46,XX patient with 17α-hydroxylase deficiency will?
No estrogen synthesis → no secondary sexual development.
5. Expected hormone set in 17α deficiency (amenorrhea, HTN, ↑Na⁺, low 17-OH-progesterone)?
Low cortisol → ↑ACTH; block → ↑DOC and ↓sex steroids.
6. 17α-hydroxylase is required to synthesize cortisol and?
The 17-hydroxylation step feeds both cortisol and sex-steroid pathways.
7. Serum potassium in 17α-hydroxylase deficiency is typically?
↑DOC (mineralocorticoid) → Na⁺ retention and K⁺ loss.
8. Cortisol level in 17α-hydroxylase deficiency is?
The block lowers cortisol → ↑ACTH.
9. A 46,XX patient with 17α deficiency presents at puberty with?
No estrogen synthesis → sexual infantilism.
10. Which enzyme deficiency gives hypertension with sexual infantilism?
The classic 17α picture: HTN + absent sex hormones.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 20 ↗

Aromatase (CYP19A1) deficiency

Cannot convert androgens → estrogens. OH question: 14-yr-old girl, amenorrhea, masculinized, partial defect in estradiol production, normal Na/glucose/cortisol → the deficient enzyme is aromatase. (Aromatase inhibitors — anastrozole/letrozole — exploit this same step in breast cancer.)
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.23 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Aromatase (CYP19A1) catalyzes?
Aromatization of androgens to estrogens.
2. Aromatase deficiency in a 46,XX fetus causes?
Androgens can't be converted to estrogen → they virilize the fetus and mother.
3. Effect of aromatase deficiency on stature and bone?
Estrogen normally closes epiphyses and maintains bone — its loss → tall + osteoporotic.
4. Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole) are used to treat?
They block estrogen production in ER⁺ breast cancer.
5. A girl with amenorrhea, masculinization, low estradiol, and normal cortisol/Na⁺ is deficient in?
Isolated defect in estrogen production points to aromatase.
6. Placental aromatase deficiency during pregnancy causes?
Fetal androgens accumulate and virilize the mother.
7. Estrogen is required for which skeletal event?
Its absence → tall stature (unfused plates) and osteoporosis.
8. Aromatase deficiency in males causes?
Estrogen matters for the male skeleton too.
9. Aromatase converts androstenedione to?
Androstenedione → estrone; testosterone → estradiol.
10. Which drugs inhibit aromatase (used in breast cancer)?
Aromatase inhibitors lower estrogen in ER⁺ disease.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 16 ↗

5α-reductase & finasteride

5α-reductase converts testosterone → dihydrotestosterone (DHT). Deficiency → 46,XY with ambiguous genitalia that virilize at puberty. ⭐ OH question: finasteride inhibits 5α-reductase (used for BPH and male-pattern hair loss).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.22 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. 5α-reductase converts testosterone to?
DHT is the more potent androgen for external genitalia/prostate.
2. A 46,XY child with 5α-reductase deficiency has genitalia that?
'Penis at 12' — testosterone surge virilizes at puberty.
3. Finasteride inhibits which enzyme?
It blocks testosterone → DHT conversion.
4. Clinical uses of finasteride?
DHT drives prostate growth and androgenetic alopecia.
5. DHT (vs testosterone) is the principal androgen for?
Testosterone → Wolffian ducts; DHT → external genitalia/prostate/hair.
6. In 5α-reductase deficiency, internal male (Wolffian) structures are?
Testosterone builds Wolffian ducts; DHT builds external genitalia.
7. Lab pattern in 5α-reductase deficiency?
The conversion T→DHT is blocked.
8. The type-2 5α-reductase isoenzyme predominates in the?
Type 2 is the prostate/genital isoform (finasteride target).
9. Which drug inhibits BOTH type 1 and type 2 5α-reductase?
Dutasteride is the dual inhibitor.
10. Which is the more potent androgen at the receptor?
DHT binds the androgen receptor more avidly.

Thyroid

concept🎥 Office hour · Slide 14 ↗

Thyroid hormone synthesis (HPT axis)

TRH (hypothalamus) → TSH (anterior pituitary) → T₃/T₄ (thyroid), with negative feedback from T₃/T₄. Synthesis steps: NIS traps iodide (basolateral) → pendrin moves it apically → TPO does organification & coupling on thyroglobulin. ⭐ OH question: TRH stimulates release of TSH from the anterior pituitary.
📄 Source lecture · Thyroid Anatomy/Histology/Biochem · p.33 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Correct order of the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis?
TRH (hypothalamus) → TSH (pituitary) → T3/T4 (thyroid).
2. Iodide is trapped into the follicular cell by?
NIS is a basolateral secondary-active transporter.
3. Organification (adding iodide to thyroglobulin tyrosines) is catalyzed by?
TPO oxidizes iodide and couples MIT/DIT.
4. Which is the biologically active thyroid hormone?
T4 is the prohormone; peripheral deiodination to T3 gives activity.
5. TRH stimulates the release of TSH from?
Office-hour question — TRH → anterior-pituitary TSH.
6. Thyroglobulin is synthesized by and stored where?
Follicular cells make thyroglobulin and store iodinated hormone in colloid.
7. Coupling of two DIT (diiodotyrosine) residues yields?
DIT+DIT → T4; MIT+DIT → T3 (via TPO).
8. Which hormone is secreted in greater quantity by the thyroid?
Mostly T4; T3 is largely made peripherally from T4.
9. Thyroid hormones travel in blood mainly bound to?
TBG carries most T4/T3; only free hormone is active.
10. TSH stimulates the thyroid follicular cell via which second messenger?
TSH receptor is a Gs-coupled GPCR → cAMP.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 9 ↗

Iodide handling & goitrogens

OH question: vegan on unprocessed cassava (a goitrogen — thiocyanate) with hypothyroidism, goiter, ↑TSH, ↓T₄. Thiocyanate competitively blocks the Na⁺/I⁻ symporter (NIS) → impaired basolateral iodide transport.
📄 Source lecture · Thyroid Anatomy/Histology/Biochem · p.28 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Unprocessed cassava causes hypothyroidism because thiocyanate?
Cassava is a goitrogen — thiocyanate competitively inhibits NIS.
2. In the office-hour vegan/cassava case, the directly impaired process was?
NIS (basolateral iodide transport) is blocked.
3. Perchlorate and pertechnetate inhibit thyroid function by?
They are competitive NIS (iodide-uptake) inhibitors.
4. The Wolff-Chaikoff effect is?
A protective autoregulatory shutdown with excess iodide.
5. Pendrin transports iodide across which membrane of the follicular cell?
NIS = basolateral in; pendrin = apical out into colloid.
6. Methimazole and propylthiouracil inhibit which enzyme?
Thioamides block organification/coupling by TPO.
7. Which additional action does propylthiouracil (PTU) have?
PTU also inhibits 5'-deiodinase — useful in thyroid storm.
8. Iodine-induced hyperthyroidism (e.g., in nodular goiter) is called the?
Jod-Basedow = iodine excess → hyperthyroidism in autonomous tissue.
9. Radioactive iodine (I-131) is taken up by the thyroid via?
NIS concentrates I-131, which then destroys the tissue.
10. Amiodarone can cause thyroid dysfunction because it?
Its high iodine load perturbs thyroid function.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 15 ↗

Thyroid hormone mechanism of action

T₃/T₄ act via intracellular (nuclear) receptors → gene transcription, not surface second messengers. A classic transcriptional target is Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (drives ↑BMR/heat). ⭐ OH question: to assay thyroid-hormone activity in tissue, measure Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase mRNA (not cAMP/Ca²⁺/PLC — those are peptide-hormone readouts).
📄 Source lecture · Thyroid Anatomy/Histology/Biochem · p.44 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Thyroid hormone acts through which receptor type?
T3 binds nuclear receptors and changes transcription.
2. A classic transcriptional target of T3 that raises BMR is?
↑Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase drives increased oxygen use/heat.
3. To assay thyroid-hormone ACTIVITY in a tissue, the best readout is?
Nuclear-receptor action → measure a transcriptional target (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase mRNA).
4. Which hormone, in contrast to T3, signals via cAMP?
Peptide hormones like TSH use surface receptors/second messengers; T3 does not.
5. Peripheral conversion of T4 to active T3 is done by?
Deiodinases remove iodine to make the active T3.
6. Most conversion of T4 to active T3 occurs in?
Peripheral 5'-deiodinase generates most circulating T3.
7. Reverse T3 (rT3) is?
Inner-ring deiodination gives inactive rT3.
8. Thyroid hormone raises basal metabolic rate largely by increasing?
More ATP-consuming ion pumping → heat and ↑BMR.
9. The thyroid hormone receptor binds DNA at?
It is a nuclear (intracellular) transcription factor.
10. Which is more potent at the receptor?
T3 is the active hormone; T4 is a prohormone.
buzzword🎥 Office hour · Slide 21 ↗

Graves vs Hashimoto

Graves = hyperthyroidism from stimulating anti-TSH-receptor antibodies (TSI) — exophthalmos, pretibial myxedema, diffuse goiter. Hashimoto = autoimmune hypothyroidism (anti-TPO / anti-thyroglobulin antibodies). ⭐ OH question: confirm Graves by testing for anti-TSH-receptor antibodies.
📄 Source lecture · Thyroid Anatomy/Histology/Biochem · p.48 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Graves disease is caused by antibodies against?
Thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins activate the TSH receptor.
2. Hashimoto thyroiditis antibodies are directed against?
Anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies.
3. Which sign is specific to Graves (not general hyperthyroidism)?
Autoantibody-driven orbital/dermal changes are Graves-specific.
4. The thyroid state in Hashimoto thyroiditis is?
Autoimmune destruction → primary hypothyroidism.
5. To confirm Graves disease, test for?
TSH-receptor antibodies (TSI) confirm Graves.
6. Radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) in Graves disease is?
TSH-receptor stimulation drives diffuse uptake.
7. Hashimoto thyroiditis histology shows?
Chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis with Hürthle-cell change.
8. Hashimoto thyroiditis increases the risk of which malignancy?
Chronic autoimmune inflammation predisposes to thyroid lymphoma.
9. Graves ophthalmopathy (exophthalmos) results from?
Immune-mediated orbital tissue expansion.
10. Neonatal Graves disease occurs because?
Transplacental TSI transiently stimulate the neonatal thyroid.

Insulin & POMC

pathway🎥 Office hour · Slide 7 ↗

Insulin processing

Preproinsulin → (signal peptide cleaved) → proinsulin → insulin + C-peptide. Proinsulin folds with disulfide bonds, then PC1/PC2 excise the connecting C-peptide. ⭐ C-peptide is co-secreted 1:1 with endogenous insulin → distinguishes endogenous insulin (C-peptide ↑) from exogenous injection (C-peptide ↓).
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.30 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. Correct sequence of insulin biosynthesis?
Signal peptide removed → proinsulin folds → C-peptide excised.
2. What is removed when proinsulin becomes mature insulin?
Prohormone convertases cut out the connecting C-peptide.
3. C-peptide is clinically useful to?
It is co-secreted 1:1 with endogenous insulin only.
4. A patient with factitious hypoglycemia from injecting insulin will have?
Exogenous insulin suppresses endogenous secretion → low C-peptide.
5. Mature insulin consists of?
Disulfide-linked A and B chains after C-peptide removal.
6. Insulin is stored in β-cell granules complexed with?
Zinc stabilizes stored insulin hexamers.
7. Glucose triggers insulin release by closing which channel?
↑ATP closes K-ATP → depolarization → Ca²⁺ influx → secretion.
8. Sulfonylureas stimulate insulin secretion by?
They close K-ATP directly → β-cell depolarization.
9. Proinsulin is cleaved to insulin in the?
Prohormone convertases process it within the granule.
10. GLUT2 on the pancreatic β-cell functions to?
GLUT2 is the high-Km glucose sensor.
pathway🎥 Office hour · Slide 7 ↗

POMC processing

POMC is cleaved tissue-specifically by PC1/PC2 into ACTH (→ α-MSH + CLIP), β-lipotropin (→ γ-MSH, β-endorphin), and N-terminal fragment. ⭐ OH annotation: POMC → ACTH → α-MSH → MC4R ties adrenal output to appetite control.
📄 Source lecture · Hormone Synthesis · p.35 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. POMC is a precursor that is cleaved into?
Tissue-specific cleavage yields these peptides.
2. α-MSH (from ACTH cleavage) reduces appetite by acting on?
α-MSH → MC4R → satiety.
3. Which enzymes cleave POMC (and proinsulin)?
PC1/PC2 process both prohormones.
4. Why do Addison disease and ectopic-ACTH syndromes cause hyperpigmentation?
High ACTH/POMC → ↑MSH activity on skin melanocytes.
5. Which of the following is derived from POMC?
β-endorphin, ACTH, MSH, and β-lipotropin all come from POMC.
6. ACTH stimulates the adrenal cortex to make cortisol via?
ACTH → Gs → cAMP → ↑StAR/steroidogenesis.
7. β-endorphin (a POMC product) acts as?
It binds opioid receptors.
8. POMC is produced mainly in the?
Corticotrophs cleave POMC into ACTH etc.
9. Loss-of-function POMC mutations cause?
No α-MSH (satiety/pigment) and no ACTH → the triad.
10. Which peptides are cleaved from POMC?
All are POMC-derived.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 18 ↗

Insulin receptor, GLUT4 & insulin resistance

Insulin receptor = receptor tyrosine kinase → IRS → PI3K/Akt → GLUT4 translocation in muscle/fat. ⭐ OH question: obese patient with high fasting glucose — the mechanism is inhibition of GLUT4 transporters (↓PI3K/Akt signaling = insulin resistance). GLUT4 is the only insulin-dependent transporter (brain = GLUT1/3, liver = GLUT2).
📄 Source lecture · Obesity & Metabolic Syndrome · p.25 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. The insulin receptor is a?
α₂β₂ tetramer with intrinsic tyrosine kinase.
2. The metabolic effects of insulin (glucose uptake) are mediated by?
PI3K/Akt drives GLUT4 to the membrane.
3. Which glucose transporter is insulin-DEPENDENT?
GLUT4 (skeletal muscle, adipose) requires insulin signaling.
4. In obesity/insulin resistance, elevated blood glucose is partly due to?
Insulin resistance blunts GLUT4 recruitment.
5. The brain takes up glucose using which (insulin-independent) transporter(s)?
Neurons use GLUT1/3; the liver uses GLUT2 — all insulin-independent.
6. The mitogenic/growth branch of insulin signaling uses?
Ras/MAPK drives growth; PI3K/Akt drives metabolism.
7. The insulin receptor phosphorylates IRS proteins on which residues?
It is a tyrosine kinase.
8. In skeletal muscle, insulin promotes?
Insulin is anabolic/storage-promoting.
9. Which transporter does the liver use for glucose (insulin-independent)?
GLUT2 in liver/β-cells is bidirectional and insulin-independent.
10. Metformin improves insulin sensitivity mainly by?
It is an insulin sensitizer, not a secretagogue.
practice🎥 Office hour · Slide 11 ↗

Insulin shock (hypoglycemia)

OH question: T1DM patient took his rapid-acting insulin (lispro) then skipped the meal → confusion, tremor, diaphoresis = insulin shock. ⭐ Labs show glucose ≈ 60 mg/dL (hypoglycemia); treat with fast glucose.
📄 Source lecture · Diabetes & Endocrine Pancreas · p.86 ↗
🧠 Test yourself · 10 questions
1. A T1DM patient injects rapid-acting insulin then skips the meal. Most likely lab finding?
Unopposed insulin without food → hypoglycemia ('insulin shock').
2. Which is a rapid-acting insulin analog?
Lispro/aspart/glulisine are rapid-acting.
3. Insulin shock refers to?
Neuroglycopenic + adrenergic symptoms from low glucose.
4. Immediate treatment of a CONSCIOUS hypoglycemic patient?
Give glucose (IM/IV glucagon or IV dextrose if unconscious).
5. During hypoglycemia, which counter-regulatory hormones rise?
All counter-regulatory hormones increase to restore glucose.
6. Which is the FIRST hormone to respond to acute hypoglycemia?
Glucagon (then epinephrine) is the rapid first defense.
7. Adrenergic symptoms of hypoglycemia include?
Catecholamine surge produces these warning signs.
8. Neuroglycopenic symptoms of hypoglycemia include?
Brain glucose deprivation → CNS dysfunction.

Glargine/detemir/degludec provide steady basal levels.
